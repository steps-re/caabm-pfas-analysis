A first-principles analysis of coagulant-assisted algae-bubble foam fractionation for PFAS removal

Michael German, Steps Ventures  ·  an outside mechanistic reading of Ni, Duan, Sun, Mai, Xiao & Deng (2026)

Subject paper Environ. Eng. Sci., doi:10.1177/15579018261469619 Version 1.0 · 2026-07-19 Code + data github.com/steps-re/caabm-pfas-analysis
Abstract

Ni et al. (2026) report a coagulant-assisted algae-bubble matrix (CAABM) that removes per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) using oxygen bubbles generated in situ by microalgal photosynthesis, achieving 7–14% removal in 97 minutes and a reported rate enhancement of up to 428× over prior algal methods. This note reconstructs the governing physics from first principles and reconciles it quantitatively against the authors' own supplementary data. Three conclusions follow. First, the process is flotation, not adsorption: the air-water interface is the sorbent, and the algae manufacture and stabilize it. Second, air-water interfacial adsorption falls roughly threefold per carbon, placing short-chain PFBA about 50× below PFOA, so foam fractionation is physically barred from removing PFBA; the coagulant works by switching the mechanism to a nearly chain-length-independent electrostatic capture, which the authors' own PFOA/PFBA removal ratio (~1.2×, versus the ~50× foam fractionation demands) confirms. Third, cell surface charge is the decisive selection variable, as the Chlorella (−24.5 mV, 0% removal) versus Ankistrodesmus (−13.8 mV, 4–14%) contrast and the pDADMAC over-dose reversal both demonstrate. We recast the authors' proposed multi-decade search for a better organism as a deterministic screen over four measurable traits, provide a reproducible scoring function and candidate shortlist, and note that the screen retrodicts the observed Ankistrodesmus-over-Chlorella result. All computations and extracted data are openly available.

1The system, in mechanistic terms

Reduced to its physics, the device is a flotation process, not a sorbent bed. Removal is not the algae adsorbing PFAS. The authors' batch tests settle this: living cells, dead cells, glass wool, and cells-on-wool all give effluent/influent ratios Ct/C0 ≥ 1 on a rotator[1]. With no accumulating bubbles, nothing is removed. Removal appears only in the illuminated column, where photosynthesis generates O2 bubbles in place.

So the true adsorbent is the air-water interface itself. The algae do three jobs: they manufacture interface (O2 bubbles) from sunlight with no aeration energy; they act as particles that stabilize the froth; and, once charge-neutralized, they and the coagulant flocs ferry PFAS that could not otherwise reach the interface. The controlling framework is the flotation capture product,

P = Pcollision · Pattachment · (1 − Pdetachment)
the probability a target is captured and retained (Sutherland 1948[17]; Mika & Fuerstenau 1968[18]; consolidated in Nguyen & Schulze 2004[7]). Every lever below acts on one of these three terms.

A subtlety makes surface chemistry decisive. In the thin bubble/water/cell film the van der Waals force is also repulsive, because air's Hamaker constant is near zero, so once the electrical double layers repel, the hydrophobic attraction is the only force left pulling a cell onto a bubble[9][10]. And three charged actors are all negative at once: the bubble surface (ζ ≈ −35 mV[6]), the cell, and the PFAS head group. Everything repels everything. That framing explains what follows.

2The chain-length wall

PFAS are surfactants; foam fractionation removes them because they adsorb at the air-water interface. Equilibrium loading follows the Gibbs isotherm, which in the dilute (linear) regime relevant here reduces to a partition coefficient:

Γ = −(1 / mRT) (dγ / d ln C)  →  Γ = Kia Cw
Γ = interfacial surface excess (mol m⁻²); Kia = air-water interfacial adsorption coefficient (length). At ug/L concentrations Kia sits at its constant maximum[2].

Kia is where chain length enters, and steeply. Lyu et al. (Brusseau group) measured, for perfluorocarboxylates, log Kia rising ~0.46–0.56 per carbon (×3 per carbon), anchored at Kia(PFOA) = 0.00285 cm[2]; the accumulation of long-chain PFAS at the interface is directly observed[3]. The thermodynamic root is that each –CF2– contributes ~−5.1 kJ/mol of adsorption free energy, about twice a –CH2– (−2.6 kJ/mol)[4][5]. Carried through the surface excess, PFBA sits ~50× below PFOA (36–90× across the measured slope range).

The result that needs no assumptions The absolute ceilings carry order-of-magnitude uncertainty; the ratio does not, because O2 rate, bubble size, and the Kia calibration all cancel. If foam fractionation were the mechanism, PFOA must out-remove PFBA by ~50× (36–90×). The authors observe 1.05× (algae only) and 1.23× (with coagulant). Foam fractionation is therefore quantitatively excluded as the PFBA removal path.

The PFOA ceiling (0.2–34%) comfortably brackets the observed 4.4%: long-chain removal is consistent with photosynthetic-bubble foam fractionation near its interfacial-area-limited ceiling. Short-chain removal is not. That split is the entire story.

3What the coagulant actually does

Coagulation alone is a weak PFAS remover, and weakest on short chains: Xiao, Simcik & Gulliver report ≤20% for PFOA/PFOS with alum or ferric[11]. Adding a cationic partner changes the regime: Maroli et al. take short-chain PFBS past 40% and long chains past 80% with ferric plus a cationic surfactant[12]. The authors' 1 ppm pDADMAC and 100 ppm FeCl3 invoke that same charge-pairing mechanism. Critically, the coagulant does not sharpen the chain-length dependence, it erases it:

The dose-response is the mechanism, in one curve At 100 ppm, pDADMAC performs worse than no coagulant at all (1.8 / 1.3%). This is restabilization: past the isoelectric point the cell surface flips positive, and like-charge repulsion returns, now against the bubble and the excess free polycation. Under-dose and over-dose both fail; the optimum sits at zero net charge. The removal knob is a peak to locate, not a monotone to push, and it is not yet mapped.

4Why Chlorella failed and Ankistrodesmus worked

The two species differ in one property that matters, and the authors' zeta data isolates it:

Ankistrodesmus falcatus−13.8 mV4–14%4–10%

In flotation the attachment barrier is set by the thin-film disjoining pressure, whose electrostatic term scales, at large separation, with the product of the two surface potentials[9][7]:

Wedl(D)  ∞  −2 ε0εκ2 ψ1ψ2 e−κD   (large D)
Israelachvili Eq. 14.61[9]. Against a common partner (the −35 mV bubble, or a floc), the repulsion scales with the cell potential ψcell.

Swapping Chlorella for Ankistrodesmus cuts that repulsive term by 1.8× on the linear scaling, up to 3.2× for a symmetric double-layer overlap. Because attachment probability falls roughly exponentially with the barrier, a ~2× taller barrier is more than enough to drive Pattachment from "works" to "zero" (see dlvo_barrier.py: a 1–2 order-of-magnitude suppression). Chlorella is not marginally worse; it is on the wrong side of the exponential. The rising effluent TOC (0.39 → 4.47 mg/L over seven days[1]) adds the second penalty the ideal organism must avoid: secreted EPS and organic acids that compete for neutralized sites and re-load negative charge.

5The 428×, stated so it survives review

The headline enhancements are real but they are rate ratios, not extents. The residence time here is 1.6 h; the bioaccumulation studies compared against ran 72–336 h[1]. Decompose it:

enhancement = (extent ratio) × (time ratio)
PFBA: 428× ≈ (~3× extent) × (~150× speed). PFOA: 21× is essentially all speed — the PFOA extent (13.9%) is below several prior reports (e.g. Scenedesmus quadricauda, 58%[1]).

The defensible claim is therefore kinetic: self-generated bubbles deliver comparable or lower single-pass extent, far faster and with no aeration energy. That is a strong position for a continuous or staged process and will not be picked apart the way "428× better removal" would. Data in ni2026_literature_comparison.csv.

A correction to the outlook's framing Famously hydrophobic self-floaters like Botryococcus braunii are tempting, but a strong photosynthetic O2 producer is now a first-order requirement, and Botryococcus is slow-growing with low O2 output. Trait 3 demotes it. The target is a fast, high-O2, non-toxic organism that also sits near zero charge, not the single most hydrophobic cell in nature.

7A deterministic screen, not a 20-year hunt

Because all four traits are measurable, the search is a ranking function over existing culture collections (UTEX, CCAP, NIES, NCMA), not an open-ended discovery. A concrete, reproducible rubric (species_screen.py):

S = 100 · Gtoxin · ( 0.35·fcharge + 0.25·fhydro + 0.20·fbubble + 0.20·flowEPS )
  • fcharge = 1 − |ζ| / 40 mV, clamped, in the target matrix. Highest weight: the gate the data proved.
  • fhydro = θ / 90° (water contact angle), or MATH% proxy.
  • fbubble = normalized specific growth rate × areal O2 evolution.
  • flowEPS = 1 − normalized effluent-TOC release.
  • Gtoxin = 0 if toxigenic, else 1.

Weights encode the evidence ranking above; re-fit once >2 strains are run through the actual column. The point is a written, reproducible screen that ranks any new strain from five measurements.

How to read the screen Among green algae with complete measured data, Ankistrodesmus falcatus leads (60.2), and it is the species that actually worked in the CAABM system, so the screen is validated against a real result. Its measured hydrophobicity (θw = 85.6°) is the reason: hydrophobic attraction is the sole force pulling a cell onto a bubble (§1), so a hydrophobic, modestly-charged cell is exactly right. Chlorella sorokiniana is the strongest practical green pick on everything except its own (unmeasured) contact angle. Species ranked higher on gaps (Chlorococcum, Nannochloropsis) are provisional until their zeta and contact angle are measured. Cyanobacteria (Anabaena) score well on pure surface physics but are excluded in practice by the toxin/regulatory gate the whole system is built to avoid.
On the data The Ozkan & Berberoglu zeta-plus-hydrophobicity dataset[13] — flagged in earlier drafts as the top missing acquisition — was recovered at HIGH confidence from the first author's open-access dissertation (the source of the paywalled papers) and now supplies single-method values for 10 species. One caveat travels with it: zeta was measured in each species' native medium, so the three diatoms (saltwater, ionic strength ~28× higher) have compressed double layers and their charge is not directly comparable to the freshwater greens.

Can we predict surface hydrophobicity from the genome and skip the measurement?

Tempting, because cell-surface hydrophobicity is largely a cell-wall property, and algaenan (a resistant aliphatic wall biopolymer) is a plausible molecular proxy that can be read from the genome. We tested it (surface_predictor.py) against the measured contact angles. The answer is no, and cleanly so:

An honest negative result Algaenan-bearing species average 60° contact angle; species without it average 64° — no signal. The apparent effect is one outlier (Botryococcus braunii, 112°); remove it and algaenan-bearing species average 42°, lower than the rest. Glycoprotein-walled Ankistrodesmus is hydrophobic (86°) with no algaenan at all. And within a single strain, growth phase alone moves contact angle ~47° (Xia et al. 2017), swamping any wall-type effect. So surface hydrophobicity must be measured, not inferred from wall class — and a genome-based predictor would first need quantitative algaenan content, growth-phase-controlled measurements, and more than the ~5 species that currently have both a sequenced genome and a measured surface. That is the honest state, and it is more useful than a predictor that looks confident and is wrong.

8Suggested next steps, in order

  1. Map the coagulant dose-response. Run pDADMAC and FeCl3 as a fine dose series against measured zeta and locate the isoelectric optimum. Likely a larger near-term gain than any new species, and it directly tests the thesis: peak removal should coincide with zero net zeta.
  2. Use the column as its own selection engine. Feed mixed environmental communities (surface microlayer, foam, bark biofilm) and serially re-inoculate from the floated fraction. Flotation becomes the selection pressure; sequence the survivors after a dozen cycles. Months, not decades.
  3. Close the remaining data gaps, then run the head-to-head. The Ozkan dataset is now in (10 species). What is left is measuring the few high-ranked gap species (Chlorella sorokiniana's own contact angle; Chlorococcum and Nannochloropsis zeta + contact angle), then running Chlorella sorokiniana and Scenedesmus dimorphus against the CAABM-validated Ankistrodesmus in the actual column. Two more column runs turn the rubric weights from assumed to fitted.
  4. Pursue extent through staging, not chemistry. Single-pass extent is modest by design; a short cascade or recycle loop compounds it while preserving the speed and zero-aeration-energy advantages.

9Open questions and caveats

  • The absolute ceiling rests on an O2 rate spanning 16× and an assumed bubble size. The ratio argument does not, so the mechanism conclusion stands even where the absolute ceilings do not.
  • Fate of captured PFAS: foam that drains back re-releases it. Confirming that PFAS leaves with collected foam or biomass, rather than redistributing, matters for any throughput claim.
  • The lipid-rich-biomass disposal argument is attractive but unquantified here; it deserves a mass balance.
  • Several cross-species surface values could not be verified past paywalls; the shortlist uses only verified sources, and unverified encodings (fbubble, flowEPS) are coarse literature estimates, flagged as such in the data.
  • This analysis treats the reported values as correct and reasons from them; if any headline number shifts, the arithmetic travels with it.

Methods

Data extraction. All experimental values were read from the machine-readable supplementary data of Ni et al. (2026)[1] (removal curves, zeta-vs-pH, effluent TOC, cell counts, and the Fig. 6 literature comparison) and transcribed into the CSV files in /data with per-row provenance. No raw supplementary files are redistributed; only the numeric values used in this analysis are reported, as is standard for a citing work.

Interfacial model. Surface excess is computed as Γ = KiaCw, with Kia from the Brusseau-group per-carbon regression anchored at the measured PFOA value[2]. The foam-fractionation ceiling equates the PFAS removal rate to Γ times the bubble-interfacial-area generation rate 3Qg/rb, with Qg from per-cell photosynthetic O2 evolution[19]. It is an explicit upper bound; ranges propagate the stated input uncertainties.

Electrostatics. Attachment-barrier scaling uses the constant-potential double-layer interaction (Israelachvili Eq. 14.61[9]) with measured cell zeta as the surface-potential proxy; attachment-probability suppression is illustrated with an Arrhenius factor, presented as scaling, not a fitted prediction.

Reproducibility. All numbers in this note are regenerated by standard-library Python scripts in /code (interfacial_adsorption.py, flotation_ceiling.py, dlvo_barrier.py, species_screen.py, surface_predictor.py). No third-party dependencies. The species screen draws on cited literature datasets (surface charge, hydrophobicity, growth/O2, EPS, taxonomy, cell-wall biochemistry) with per-value provenance in data/species_sources.md; a broader dataset catalog for this and future work is in DATASETS.md.
